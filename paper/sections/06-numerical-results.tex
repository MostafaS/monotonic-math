\section{Numerical Results and Sensitivity Analysis}
\label{sec:numerical-results}

Section~\ref{sec:formal-invariants} established the floor-monotonicity property as a structural theorem and Section~\ref{sec:economic-analysis} derived closed-form characterizations of the adversarial decision problem, the LP half-life, and the calling and MEV-bound equilibria. This section reports the numerical operating envelope of the protocol over a 36-month horizon, sweeps the four free design parameters, and confronts the analytics with a Hardhat v3 invariant-test suite that asserts the floor property on the actual compiled bytecode of the token, treasury, and revenue router (with a \texttt{MockStable} ERC-20 standing in for the backing stablecoin counterparty in the fast invariant lane and the deployed mainnet stable on the fork lane); V4-mediated cases (LPBuy, LPSell, CollectFees) are exercised against the deployed mainnet PoolManager on the \texttt{v0.1.1-paper-v1} fork lane and against semantics-equivalent mocks in the fast invariant lane. The reproducibility artifact (Section~\ref{sec:numerical-reproducibility}) regenerates every figure and table in this section from a single command, and pins the version cited in the abstract.

\subsection{Methodology: a two-track simulation stack}
\label{sec:numerical-methodology}

We adopt a two-track simulation strategy whose central design discipline is that the two tracks must produce numerically agreeing results on every shared scenario, with divergence treated as a paper-stopping bug.

\paragraph{Track A: Python analytics.} A vectorized NumPy simulator computes the deterministic closed-form recurrence and the lognormal-stochastic-revenue Monte Carlo bands; a single canonical-month-12 state is also computed in 60-digit \texttt{Decimal} arithmetic to anchor Theorem~\ref{thm:bank-run}'s numerical example. An on-chain-mirroring integer reference implementation (with the truncation residual of Lemma~\ref{lem:integer-redemption}) lives on the Track-B side and is exercised by the invariant suite; the Track-A simulator does not duplicate the integer semantics. Each Monte Carlo scenario runs $10^4$ sample paths per (scenario $\times$ sweep-cell) combination. Track A is the source of every figure and table in this section.

\paragraph{Track B: Hardhat v3 invariant tests.} A handler contract issues bounded random calls to the deployed protocol's \texttt{routeRevenue}, \texttt{swap}, \texttt{redeem}, and \texttt{collectFees} entry points. The suite is written as Solidity \texttt{.t.sol} files using \texttt{forge-std}-style assertions but is executed by Hardhat v3's native EDR (Ethereum Development Runtime) rather than by Foundry's \texttt{forge}; the project is configured through \texttt{hardhat.config.ts} alone, with no \texttt{foundry.toml}. After every state transition, four invariant assertions are checked: (i) $\Floor_{\text{new}} \geq \Floor_{\text{old}}$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:floor-monotonicity}); (ii) $\Tres \geq 0$ (treasury solvency); (iii) $\Sup \leq \Sup_0$ (no post-genesis minting); (iv) $\Tres \geq \Sup \cdot \min_t \Floor(t)$ (redemption-solvency lower bound). The runner is configured with $1{,}000$ stateless and $1{,}000$ stateful (depth $50$) random sequences on the fast gate; $10{,}000$ stateless and $100{,}000$ stateful (depth $200$) sequences on the \texttt{v0.1.1-paper-v1} tag gate, configured via the \texttt{M2\_FUZZ\_RUNS} / \texttt{M2\_INVARIANT\_RUNS} / \texttt{M2\_INVARIANT\_DEPTH} env knobs. GitHub Actions CI regresses the fast gate on every push to \texttt{main} and every PR, and the full gate on the \texttt{v0.1.1-paper-v1} tag. A divergence between Tracks A and B on any agreed scenario halts the paper revision.

\paragraph{Agreement gate.} A reconciliation script compares Track A's CSV outputs against a TypeScript reference model of the protocol's deterministic recurrence (\texttt{contracts/scripts/agreement\_gate.ts}), and verifies tolerance-band agreement (\texttt{ABS\_TOL} $= 10^{-3}$ in fee-free stable units, \texttt{REL\_TOL} $= 10^{-9}$) on $(\Tres, \Sup, \Lt, \Ls)$ trajectories across a shared deterministic baseline scenario. The cross-check is designed to catch specification drift between the paper, the Python recurrence, and the Solidity reference (e.g., rounding direction on the integer-arithmetic redemption residual; ordering of router buy-and-burn versus treasury deposit) and is the single most important quality discipline in the simulation pipeline.

\paragraph{Fee-free curve math vs.\ with-fees mode.} For legibility, the deterministic baseline scenarios and figures reported in this paper are presented under \emph{fee-free curve math}: each $50/50$ revenue split contributes its full $B = R/2$ to the LP's stable reserve, with the $0.10\%$ buy fee ignored. We use this convention to keep the closed-form recurrence (and the table values) interpretable by hand. The with-fees mode---in which the $0.10\%$ buy fee is deducted on input, folded into $\Phis$, and realized at the next \texttt{collectFees}---is the implementation Track A will run against Track B in the agreement gate. The Track-A/Track-B tolerance-band agreement claim therefore concerns the with-fees output of Track A against the on-bytecode invariant-fuzz behavior of Track B; the with-fees correction to each table entry is $O(f_b) = O(10^{-3})$ and is reported separately in Appendix~\ref{app:with-fees}.

\subsection{Sensitivity sweep: revenue rate}
\label{sec:numerical-revenue}

We sweep monthly revenue $R \in \{\$25\text{k}, \$50\text{k}, \$100\text{k}, \$250\text{k}, \$500\text{k}, \$1\text{M}\}$ over a 36-month horizon. The closed-form prediction from Theorem~\ref{thm:lp-half-life} is $n_{1/2}(R) = L_{s,0}/(R/2) = 2 L_{s,0}/R$ (fee-free curve math; the $1/(1-f_b)$ correction is $O(f_b) = O(10^{-3})$), ranging from 60 months at $R = \$25\text{k}$ down to 1.5 months at $R = \$1\text{M}$. The cumulative floor at month 36 grows roughly as $\sqrt{R}$ in the LP-rich regime and saturates as the LP thins (because the marginal tokens-per-dollar of buy-and-burn falls as $\Ls$ grows).

\subsection{Sensitivity sweep: LP/vesting split}
\label{sec:numerical-lp-split}

We sweep the genesis LP allocation $\rho_{\text{LP}} \in \{50\%, 60\%, 75\%, 85\%, 90\%\}$ of total supply with the remainder going to the vesting vault. This sweep is illustrative: the canonical M\textsuperscript{2} design is $\rho_{\text{LP}} = 75\%$ (Section~\ref{sec:protocol-genesis}); other splits are reachable only by deploying a separate token. Two metrics are reported: (i) the LP half-life, which scales linearly with $\rho_{\text{LP}}$ via Theorem~\ref{thm:lp-half-life}; (ii) the floor at month 36 under baseline revenue. The Pareto trade-off is between LP longevity (higher $\rho_{\text{LP}}$ extends the tradable regime) and floor speed (lower $\rho_{\text{LP}}$ accelerates per-token floor growth at the cost of LP thinning).

\subsection{Sensitivity sweep: asymmetric fee}
\label{sec:numerical-fee}

We sweep the sell-side fee $f_s \in \{0\%, 1\%, 3\%, 5\%, 10\%\}$ while holding the buy-side fee at the design's 0.10\%. Two effects are decomposed: (i) at the swap moment, a higher $f_s$ shifts more value into the v4 fee accumulator $\Phit$, which on \texttt{collectFees} translates to a token-side burn; (ii) the holder-side disincentive on sells suppresses adversarial dump-channel use (Theorem~\ref{thm:bank-run}: $A^*$ shrinks as $f_s$ rises, because the marginal post-fee LP spot $(1 - f_s) \Spot$ drops relative to the floor sooner).

Figure~\ref{fig:fee-attribution} shows a stacked-bar decomposition of monthly floor growth into three sources: revenue-deposit (Case~1), buy-and-burn (Case~2), and \texttt{collectFees} (Case~7). At $f_s = 0$, source~(3) is dominated by the buy-side 0.10\% fee, and floor growth is driven almost entirely by sources~(1) and~(2). At $f_s = 10\%$, source~(3) becomes the dominant component under any organic-flow regime with appreciable sell volume.

\subsection{Adversarial scenarios}
\label{sec:numerical-adversarial}

Five adversarial scenarios drawn from the simulation plan exercise the protocol under coordinated holder behavior.

\paragraph{S2 (revenue stops).} Revenue $\to 0$ at month $t_{\text{stop}} \in \{6, 12, 24\}$. Floor is constant from $t_{\text{stop}}$ onward (Theorem~\ref{thm:spot-floor-convergence}~(1)); under the assumed zero organic flow, spot also stalls until any future sell triggers a \texttt{collectFees} micro-tick. The redemption channel remains fully functional; the system enters the CEF analog regime.

\paragraph{S3 (mass redemption).} 25\%/50\%/75\% of circulating supply redeems at month 12. The Track-A self-check verifies that the redemption funding ratio (the ratio of stable paid out to the floor-times-tokens-burned) equals 1.0 to within the integer-arithmetic residual; this is a sanity test for the implementation rather than a result. Floor is invariant by Lemma~\ref{lem:integer-redemption}.

\paragraph{S4 (mass dump).} The same supply fractions are dumped to the LP at month 12 instead of redeemed. The LP becomes stable-light (its $\Ls$ partially drains); the immediate spot collapses; the v4 fee accumulator fills with token-side fees; on the next \texttt{collectFees} the floor steps up by an amount predictable from the closed form of Section~\ref{sec:formal-invariants}. Figure~\ref{fig:attacker-phasediagram} shows the attacker's yield surface $\Pi^*(\alpha, N)$ alongside the saturation curve $\alpha = A^*/N$ from Theorem~\ref{thm:bank-run}.

\paragraph{S5 (adversarial-mixed).} The attacker plays the saturated mixed strategy of Theorem~\ref{thm:bank-run}~(2). The yield surface is plotted against $(\alpha, N)$ in Figure~\ref{fig:attacker-phasediagram}; the dotted curve is the optimal strategy locus.

\paragraph{S6 (organic-volume regimes).} Monthly organic sell volume $V \in \{\$10\text{k}, \$50\text{k}, \$200\text{k}, \$500\text{k}\}$. Figure~\ref{fig:organic-volume} shows the floor's growth rate as a function of $V$; at $V = \$500\text{k}$/month, the fee-collection source contributes roughly one third of the buy-and-burn source's floor-compounding effect, and parity with buy-and-burn is reached at $V \approx \$1.6\text{M}$/month under the canonical month-12 state. We report the floor's dependence on $V$ as a sweep parameter rather than fixing a single baseline organic-volume assumption.

\subsection{Monte Carlo: stochastic revenue and organic flow}
\label{sec:numerical-mc}

We replace deterministic revenue with a lognormal monthly-revenue process at the canonical mean. \emph{Revenue}: lognormal monthly draws with shape parameter $\sigma_X = 1.0$ and location calibrated so $E[R] = \$100$k/month. Organic-flow GBM and the joint Poisson-arrival/$\sigma_X$ sweep are deferred to the empirical follow-up paper, where they will be calibrated to live deployment data rather than to synthetic comparables.

Figure~\ref{fig:montecarlo-bands} reports the floor's 5/50/95 quantile bands across $10^4$ Monte Carlo paths under lognormal monthly revenue with $\sigma_X = 1.0$ and $E[R] = \$100$k, seed \texttt{42}. The floor's 5\%-quantile path remains monotonically above $\$0.001$ across the entire 36-month horizon; the median path tracks a few percent below the deterministic baseline (a systematic gap reflecting the median-vs-mean wedge of lognormal monthly revenue, which has median $e^{-\sigma_X^2/2} \approx 0.61$ of its mean for $\sigma_X = 1$), with the maximum absolute deviation bounded by $\sim 5\%$ over the horizon; the 95\%-quantile path benefits from the asymmetric-fee mechanism whenever the spot's volatility induces large sells.

\subsection{Reproducibility}
\label{sec:numerical-reproducibility}

The simulator codebase is released under the MIT License at Zenodo DOI \href{https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.20255141}{\texttt{10.5281/zenodo.20255141}}. The repository's top-level \texttt{make reproduce} regenerates every figure and table in this section from the deterministic seeds pinned in the configuration. The git tag \texttt{v0.1.1-paper-v1} pins the version cited; subsequent versions (post-deployment empirical follow-up) will increment the tag. The Hardhat v3 invariant-test suite (in \texttt{contracts/}) is invoked by \texttt{npx hardhat test} or the equivalent root-level \texttt{make fuzz} alias; CI runs both tracks on every PR and rejects merges that introduce divergence.

A reviewer of the paper can verify the entire numerical section as follows. Clone the repository, check out \texttt{v0.1.1-paper-v1}, install the documented Python and Hardhat v3 toolchains, and run \texttt{make verify}: this performs Track A, Track B, and the agreement gate, and writes a SHA-256 manifest of every generated artifact. A reviewer-supplied diff against the paper's figure files should reproduce the SHA-256 manifest exactly.
